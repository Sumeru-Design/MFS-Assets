Documented friction incidents — 5 across 3 projects

The brief is too thin. Figma is absorbing the cost.

Late-stage feedback friction occurs when strategic or structural feedback arrives in the final Veeva approval round. It creates timeline pressure, team tension, and rework that costs 2–3x more than the same change in round 1. Five incidents are documented below — all share the same structural cause.

What these incidents have in common

Structural causes

Strategy not locked before design begins — brief assumptions get tested in Figma. Program scope (Libre Support, VAST, Customer Care) not documented and shared with Sumeru upfront. No round-gating agreement — no shared definition of what type of feedback is appropriate per review round. No shared module tracking — fixes applied locally, not propagated globally.

What it costs Sumeru

Rework in the final round costs 2–3x more time than the same change in round 1. It creates adversarial dynamics — the Wave 2 incident is fully documented and unresolved. It absorbs senior designer time on copy edits and CTA debates rather than design decisions. It shifts accountability for strategic alignment onto Sumeru's execution team rather than the Abbott brief owners.

Prioritized recommendations — 9 actions

Fix upstream, automate downstream.

The highest-leverage changes are not in the QA plugin — they are in the brief and the review process. The plugin catches what slips through. The brief prevents it from slipping in the first place.

Priority 1 — prevent before Figma (upstream)
Program scope reference card — required brief attachment
Critical
A one-page document mapping: Libre Support (what it does / does not cover), VAST (scope, eligibility, what agents can confirm), Customer Care (replacements, billing), copay card (who can use it, what it guarantees). Would have prevented 38 comments in Nudges P2 alone. Required deliverable from Abbott before Sumeru begins any cost/coverage email project.
Round gate definition — agreed with Derek before round 1 opens
Critical
Round 1 = strategy and copy direction. Round 2 = detailed copy edits, image feedback, module-level changes. Round 3 = regulatory/compliance only (RA, Reimbursement, Clinical). Needs formal agreement with Derek and Marina — not assumed. The Wave 2 confrontation had no framework to reference. This single change would reduce late-stage friction by an estimated 60%.
Disclaimer symbol reference frame — pinned to every Figma file
Critical
A frame at the top of every file: symbol → disclaimer text → which claim it applies to. 20 comments across two waves were about asterisk vs ‡‡. If a designer sees the approved mapping in the same file before starting work, this error drops to zero. Takes 30 minutes to build once, used for every project.
Image brief checklist — per email, per audience segment
Critical
Before selecting any image: Who is this email for? Age range? Insurance type? Verified stock image (not internal Abbott employee)? 10+ image/audience mismatch comments across all 3 files. This checklist sits in the QA spreadsheet, not in Figma. Takes 5 minutes per email to fill in.
Priority 2 — plugin automation (catch before client review)
Flag "Libre Assist" not followed by approved phrase
High
Rule: any occurrence of "Libre Assist" must be followed by "a feature only in the Libre app". Flags: "the newest feature", "a feature of", "the latest feature". 12 comments across W1 and W2. Simple string match — highest-ROI plugin rule.
Flag asterisk (*) symbols in disclaimer zones near predicted language
High
20 comments across Libre Assist alone. Plugin scans footer/disclaimer text nodes for * and flags if "predicted" language appears anywhere in the email. Designer must confirm intentional use. Prevents the single most consistently flagged RA symbol error.
Flag "track" adjacent to Libre Assist / glucose language
High
RA consistently rejects "track" — Libre Assist shows predicted and actual impact, it doesn't track. "See" is the approved replacement. 3 instances in Wave 2. Simple rule: flag "track" within 20 words of "Libre Assist" or "glucose".
CTA link audit column in QA checklist
High
28 link-related comments across all 3 files. Add a column to the existing QA spreadsheet: CTA label → destination URL → confirmed Y/N. Would have caught the URL swap that reached Veeva in Wave 1 and the "where does this link go?" questions that appear in every file.
Priority 3 — workflow (reduce coordination noise)
Move handoff pings and status updates out of Figma into Workfront
Medium
Approximately 30–40% of internal comments across all files are review request pings ("@Derek please review"), status updates ("Updated", "Done"), and acknowledgments. These belong in Workfront tasks. Moving them out reduces noise by ~35% and makes substantive feedback easier to find in Figma.
Emailify pilot — 2 templates, measure HTML output vs ESP requirements
Medium
Link errors that reach Veeva are partly caused by manual handoff from Figma to the ESP (likely Salesforce Marketing Cloud). Emailify exports CTA links directly from Figma, making the URL audit programmatic. Test on 2 non-critical Nudges templates before committing to full rollout.
